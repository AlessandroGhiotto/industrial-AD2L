        \section{Introduction}
        The ADL 2025--2026 challenge targets \textit{industrial visual anomaly
        detection}: given eight product classes photographed from five views
        each, the task is to detect and localise defects at the pixel
        level. 
        Our final submission is a two-branch system: an unsupervised detector built on PatchCore~\cite{roth2022patchcore} with a
        DINOv2~\cite{oquab2024dinov2} backbone, and a supervised U-Net
        \cite{ronneberger2015unet} trained on synthetic defects.

        \textbf{Label scarcity:} We were provided with only a few labelled anomalies for each class, naturally pushing us towards an unsupervised or weakly supervised solution.\\
        \textbf{Inconsistent views:} The view IDs were not associated with a specific view angle.\\
        \textbf{Anomaly descriptions:} Each ground truth mask was accompanied by a brief description of the defect it was highlighting.
        
        
        \subsection{First attempts: reconstruction and student--teacher}
        We started with pure reconstruction baselines (vanilla and denoising
        autoencoders). The network learned to reconstruct anomalies
        almost as well as normal regions, and the anomaly heatmaps were contaminated
        by noise outside the object. The student--teacher
        paradigm~\cite{bergmann2020uninformed}, with a Wide-ResNet-50-2~\cite{Zagoruyko2016WRN} teacher
        and a ResNet-18~\cite{He2015Deep} student trained to mimic its features on normal data,
        gave better localisation than autoencodes but showed poor performance on some classes (especially class 3, see Tab.~\ref{tab:per-class-ap}) and proved more expensive to train than PatchCore.

        \subsection{BiRefNet preprocessing}
        After noticing that a lot of different models were struggling with false positives on background reflections, we decided to
        preprocesse the whole dataset with
        BiRefNet~\cite{zheng2024birefnet}: a high-resolution
        segmenter that produces
        a soft foreground mask per image. All
        subsequent models use this cleaned dataset, which removes a large
        source of false positives along the object's silhouette and in the background. Although background removal occasionally clipped parts of dark defects, this did not affect performance, as those defects were still correctly identified.


        \subsection{Pose clustering}
        Since the filename suffix \texttt{viewN} does \emph{not} consistently encode
        the same camera angle, to recover
        pose-coherent groups we mean-pool DINOv2 patch tokens into
        descriptor per image, and run $k$-means per class
        and picking $K$ by silhouette score. The
        resulting assignments are reused
        downstream to constrain CutPaste sampling and for FiLM conditioning.

        \subsection{PatchCore with DINOv2}
        PatchCore became our first competitive detector. We extract dense
        patch tokens from a frozen \texttt{vit\_base\_patch14\_reg4\_dinov2}
        at $518 \times 518$, concatenating layers $\{3, 6, 9, 11\}$ to mix
        mid-level texture and high-level semantics, then project the resulting
        $4 \cdot 768$-d features down to $384$ components with a
        randomised-SVD PCA. A greedy coreset of $\leq 10^5$ anchors is built
        per class and stored as a tensor; at inference time the anomaly score
        of each patch is the mean Euclidean distance to its $k=9$ nearest
        coreset entries. The patch grid is smoothed with a Gaussian
        ($\sigma = 1$) and bilinearly upsampled to image resolution.

        Replacing the ImageNet-pretrained Wide-ResNet of the original
        formulation~\cite{roth2022patchcore} with DINOv2 features gave a significant boost to pixel AP.
        We also ran experiments with the larger DinoV2 variants, but the measured improvement was marginal.

        \subsection{Supervised U-Net on synthetic anomalies}
        PatchCore alone leaves some clear failure modes: low-contrast
        defects whose features are too close to the normal manifold and defects which are small compared to patch size. A
        complementary supervised branch trained with explicit labels is the natural fix, but only a few real
        anomalies per class are available. We therefore turned the few
        labelled anomalies and the normal images into a synthetic
        segmentation training set.

        \textbf{Synthetic anomaly generation.}
        We build a synthetic segmentation dataset by pasting anomalous regions onto normal images. Our \texttt{ShapeTextureBank} disentangles defect \emph{shape} and \emph{texture}: shapes are sampled from both real and procedurally generated masks (e.g., warped masks, Perlin blobs, strokes), while textures are cropped from real anomalies. Synthetic defects are inserted through CutPaste-style compositing~\cite{li2021cutpaste} with geometric and photometric augmentations, optionally combining multiple defects in the same image. We additionally generate procedural motifs such as scratches, cracks, stains, and holes, selected according to the anomaly descriptions of each class. To improve realism, pasted defects are restricted to source images sharing the same pose cluster as the target sample.
        
        % \textbf{Synthetic anomaly generation.} The
        % \texttt{ShapeTextureBank} decouples a defect's \emph{shape} from its

        % $p_\text{synth}{=}0.4$ a 
        % procedural defect is drawn instead:
        % scratches, stains, indents, cracks, fragments, holes, fuzz; the motif
        % list per class is derived by keyword-matching the anomaly
        % descriptions CSV. \textbf{Pose-aware sampling} restricts every paste
        % to a source crop that shares the destination's pose cluster; this is
        % important because pasting a top-down defect onto a side view produces
        % unrealistic supervision.

        \textbf{Architecture.} The network is a U-Net with a
        \texttt{resnet34} ImageNet-pretrained encoder, four upsampling stages
        with \textbf{attention gates}~\cite{oktay2018attentionunet} on every
        skip connection, and a \textit{view-id} FiLM conditioning that lets a
        single per-class model specialise its decoder per camera angle.
        Training uses a $0.5{:}0.5$ BCE+Dice loss, masked to the BiRefNet
        foreground.
        
        \textbf{Inference.} Test-time augmentation (identity + horizontal +
        vertical flip) is averaged, the BiRefNet foreground is re-applied at
        test time, and the five views of a physical sample are
        \emph{co-amplified}: if at least one view confidently fires, the
        others are scaled by a factor proportional to the maximum confidence.

        \subsection{Ensemble}
        PatchCore and the U-Net make complementary mistakes. PatchCore is
        sharp on out-of-distribution patches but produces a ring artefact
        along the object contour and noisy spots inside textured regions;
        the U-Net is clean and shape-aware but misses rare defects.
        Before fusion, we therefore (i) clean up the PatchCore map by discarding any connected regions smaller than 50 pixels that exceed the $p_{99}$ threshold, and (ii) normalise both maps per class using the \emph{excess-above-$p_{99}$} statistic computed on good-image scores, so that background noise is mapped to $0$. The fused map
        is then
        \begin{equation}
            \label{eq:fusion}
            s_\text{fused} = \alpha\, s_\text{U-Net} + (1-\alpha)\, s_\text{PC},
        \end{equation}
        with the per-class weight $\alpha \in \{0, 0.05, \dots, 1\}$ chosen by
        grid search to maximise pooled pixel-AP on the labelled training
        anomalies of that class. The weighted sum was the most
        robust on validation.

        \paragraph{Validation protocol.}
        To select $\alpha$ and compare strategies we construct a validation from the labeled training data.  For each class, 20\% of
        the normal images are withheld; the remaining 80\% are used to rebuild a class-specific
        PatchCore bank with the same pipeline.  All labeled training anomalies are
        added to the validation pool with their pixel masks. The same methodology was used to
        evaluate the alternative fusion rules (\texttt{max},
        \texttt{geometric\_mean}, \texttt{cascaded}, \texttt{two\_tier}) and the PatchCore power
        transform $s_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\gamma}$ for
        $\gamma \in \{0.5, 0.75, 1.25, 1.5, 2.0, 2.5, 3.0\}$.
        
        \subsection{Other approaches we tried shallowly}
        We additionally ran controlled experiments with
        DRAEM~\cite{zavrtanik2021draem},
        EfficientAD~\cite{batzner2024efficientad},
        RD4AD~\cite{deng2022rd4ad},
        SimpleNet~\cite{liu2023simplenet} and the few-shot
        WinCLIP+~\cite{jeong2023winclip} detector. None of them was
        competitive with the PatchCore+U-Net pair on this dataset under our
        time budget, partly because the small per-class regime penalises
        the models that need long training (DRAEM, EfficientAD, SimpleNet, Student-Teacher),
        and partly because few-shot prompting (WinCLIP+) lacks the fine
        spatial resolution required by the pixel-AP metric. 
        We further explored several feature configurations for PatchCore, including DINOv2 CLS tokens for global semantic representations (which tended to produce hard to explain anomaly scores), Depth Anything V2~\cite{yang2024depthv2} features for shape-oriented cues (which tended to over-smooth the anomaly maps). We also investigated GrabCut-based~\cite{rother2004grabcut} post-processing of anomaly heatmaps.
        % Nevertheless, these alternatives showed several limitations. DINOv2 CLS tokens produced unstable anomaly scores, occasionally overreacting to minor local variations while assigning high scores to nominally normal samples.
        
        \section{Discussion}
        Some of the changes that improved the pixel-AP the most were:
         replacing ImageNet features with DINOv2 inside
        PatchCore,  masking out the background with
        BiRefNet end-to-end, and calibrating raw PatchCore scores by dividing each test heatmap by the 99th percentile of per-image maximum scores on normal training images, mapping distances to a consistent $[0,1]$ range across classes. The calibration step addressed the raw k-NN distances having incomparable magnitudes across classes. The main remaining weakness is on
        defects that are small or hard to replicate with our defect generation pipeline: PatchCore underweights
        them because their features are close to the normal manifold, and
        the U-Net underweights them because the synthetic distribution
        rarely matches them.
        Our analysis shows that the ensemble outperformed the U-Net alone, primarily because PatchCore provides greater sensitivity to defects which were not seen by the U-Net during training (see Fig.~\ref{fig:results}).
        \subsection{Areas of improvement}
        A natural next step would be to feed PatchCore's
        confidence as an additional input channel to the U-Net so that the
        supervised branch can learn where to focus its capacity.
        Our analysis revealed that certain classes contained defects that were significantly harder to detect, with the pistachio nut class scoring the lowest. A possible solution would be to add another network specialized in texture defects (i.e., DRAEM) to the ensemble.
