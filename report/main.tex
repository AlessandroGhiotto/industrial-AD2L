        \section{Introduction}
        The ADL 2025--2026 challenge targets \textit{industrial visual anomaly
        detection}: given eight product classes photographed from five views
        each, the task is to detect and localise defects at the pixel
        level.
        Our final submission is a two-branch system: an unsupervised detector built on PatchCore~\cite{roth2022patchcore} with a
        DINOv2~\cite{oquab2024dinov2} backbone, and a supervised U-Net
        \cite{ronneberger2015unet} trained on synthetic defects.

        \section{Development Process}

        \subsection{Problem Analysis}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Label scarcity:} We were provided with only a few labelled anomalies for each class, naturally pushing us towards an unsupervised or weakly supervised solution.
            \item \textbf{Inconsistent views:} The view IDs were not correlated with a specific view angle.
            \item \textbf{Anomaly descriptions:} Each ground truth mask was accompanied by a brief description of the defect it was highlighting.
        \end{itemize}
        \subsection{First attempts: reconstruction and student--teacher}
        We started with pure reconstruction baselines (vanilla and denoising
        autoencoders). The reconstruction loss was too coarse: the network learned to reconstruct anomalies
        almost as well as normal regions, and the anomaly heatmaps were contaminated
        by noise outside the object. The student--teacher
        paradigm~\cite{bergmann2020uninformed}, with a Wide-ResNet-50-2 teacher
        and a ResNet-18 student trained to mimic its features on normal data,
        gave better localisation but the score map still leaked heavily onto
        the background and on cluttered fixtures, which strongly suggested that
        \textbf{background removal} had to come first.

        \subsection{BiRefNet preprocessing}
        We therefore preprocessed the whole dataset with
        BiRefNet~\cite{zheng2024birefnet}: a high-resolution
        segmenter that produces
        a soft foreground mask per image. We binarise it at $0.5$, and composite the foreground over a
        constant black fill. Ground-truth anomaly masks are not touched. All
        subsequent models use this cleaned dataset, which removes a large
        source of false positives along the object's silhouette and in the background.

        \subsection{Pose clustering}
        Since the filename suffix \texttt{viewN} does \emph{not} consistently encode
        the same camera angle across different physical objects, to recover
        pose-coherent groups we mean-pool DINOv2 patch tokens into a 768-d
        descriptor per image, $L_2$-normalise, and run $k$-means per class
        sweeping $K \in [2, 8]$ and picking $K$ by silhouette score. The
        resulting assignments are reused
        downstream to constrain CutPaste sampling and for FiLM conditioning.

        \subsection{PatchCore with DINOv2}
        PatchCore became our first competitive detector. We extract dense
        patch tokens from a frozen \texttt{vit\_base\_patch14\_reg4\_dinov2}
        at $518 \times 518$, concatenating layers $\{7, 12, 16, 20\}$ to mix
        mid-level texture and high-level semantics, then project the resulting
        $4 \cdot 768$-d features down to 512 components with a
        randomised-SVD PCA. A greedy coreset of $\leq 10^5$ anchors is built
        per class and stored as a tensor; at inference time the anomaly score
        of each patch is the mean Euclidean distance to its $k=9$ nearest
        coreset entries. The patch grid is smoothed with a Gaussian
        ($\sigma = 1$) and bilinearly upsampled to image resolution. We also
        ran the large variant (\texttt{vit\_large\_patch14\_reg4\_dinov2}, $4\!\cdot\!1024$-d)
        but observed essentially no pixel-AP gain over the base model, so we
        kept the cheaper backbone.

        Replacing the ImageNet-pretrained Wide-ResNet of the original
        formulation~\cite{roth2022patchcore} with DINOv2 features gave a significant boost to pixel AP: the
        ViT representations are far less sensitive to global appearance
        variations and emphasise local structure, which is what most industrial defects amount to.

        \subsection{Supervised U-Net on synthetic anomalies}
        PatchCore alone leaves a clear failure mode: subtle, low-contrast
        defects whose features are too close to the normal manifold. A
        complementary supervised branch trained with explicit labels is the natural fix, but only a few dozen real
        anomalies per class are available. We therefore turned the few
        labelled anomalies and a large pool of normal images into a synthetic
        segmentation training set.

        \textbf{Synthetic anomaly generation.} The
        \texttt{ShapeTextureBank} decouples a defect's \emph{shape} from its
        \emph{texture}: shapes are sampled from a mixture of real masks
        (30\%), elastically warped real masks (25\%), cut-mixed pairs of real
        masks (10\%), Perlin-noise blobs (15\%), Bézier strokes (15\%) and
        random walks (5\%); textures are bbox-cropped pixels from real
        defects. At each \texttt{\_\_getitem\_\_} call on a normal image, with
        probability $p_\text{cutpaste}{=}0.3$ a defect is pasted via
        CutPaste~\cite{li2021cutpaste} with geometric and photometric jitter
        (random flip, rotation, mask dilation/erosion, brightness/contrast,
        feathered alpha-blending), and with probability $p_\text{multi}{=}0.3$
        a second crop is pasted on top. With probability
        $p_\text{synth}{=}0.4$ a purely procedural defect is drawn instead:
        scratches, stains, indents, cracks, fragments, holes, fuzz; the motif
        list per class is derived by keyword-matching the anomaly
        descriptions CSV. \textbf{Pose-aware sampling} restricts every paste
        to a source crop that shares the destination's pose cluster; this is
        important because pasting a top-down defect onto a side view produces
        unrealistic supervision. Anomalous training images, which lack a
        pose label, are assigned to the nearest labelled good image of the
        same class via $32 \times 32$ grayscale $L_2$ nearest neighbour.

        \textbf{Architecture.} The network is a U-Net with a
        \texttt{resnet34} ImageNet-pretrained encoder, four upsampling stages
        with \textbf{attention gates}~\cite{oktay2018attentionunet} on every
        skip connection, and a \textit{view-id} FiLM conditioning that lets a
        single per-class model specialise its decoder per camera angle.
        Training uses a $0.5{:}0.5$ BCE+Dice loss, masked to the BiRefNet
        foreground.

        \textbf{Inference.} Test-time augmentation (identity + horizontal +
        vertical flip) is averaged, the BiRefNet foreground is re-applied at
        test time, and the five views of a physical sample are
        \emph{co-amplified}: if at least one view confidently fires, the
        others are scaled by a factor proportional to the maximum confidence.

        \subsection{Ensemble}
        PatchCore and the U-Net make complementary mistakes. PatchCore is
        sharp on out-of-distribution patches but produces a ring artefact
        along the object contour and noisy specks inside textured regions;
        the U-Net is clean and shape-aware but conservative on rare motifs.
        Before fusion we therefore (i) morphologically open the PatchCore map
        with a $r{=}3$ kernel and drop connected components smaller than
        50 pixels above the $p_{99}$ threshold, and (ii) normalise both maps
        per class using the \emph{excess-above-$p_{99}$} statistic of
        good-image scores so that the noise floor maps to $0$. The fused map
        is then
        \begin{equation}
            \label{eq:fusion}
            s_\text{fused} = \alpha\, s_\text{U-Net} + (1-\alpha)\, s_\text{PC},
        \end{equation}
        with the per-class weight $\alpha \in \{0, 0.05, \dots, 1\}$ chosen by
        grid search to maximise pooled pixel-AP on the labelled training
        anomalies of that class. The weighted sum was the most
        robust on validation.
        \paragraph{Validation protocol.}
        To select $\alpha$ and compare strategies we build a held-out proxy
        split from the labelled training data: 20\% of the normal images are
        withheld, the remaining 80\% rebuild a class-specific PatchCore bank
        and re-estimate the $p_{99}$ calibration constant, and all labelled
        training anomalies are pooled with their pixel masks. Per-class
        $\alpha$ is then swept independently to maximise pooled pixel-AP. The
        same protocol was reused to compare the alternative fusion rules
        (\texttt{max}, \texttt{geometric\_mean}, \texttt{cascaded},
        \texttt{two\_tier}) and a PatchCore power transform
        $s_{\mathrm{PC}}^{\gamma}$.

        \subsection{Other approaches we tried shallowly}
        We additionally ran shorter experiments with
        DRAEM~\cite{zavrtanik2021draem},
        EfficientAD~\cite{batzner2024efficientad},
        RD4AD~\cite{deng2022rd4ad},
        SimpleNet~\cite{liu2023simplenet} and the few-shot
        WinCLIP+~\cite{jeong2023winclip}: none of them was competitive with
        the PatchCore+U-Net pair under our time budget, and their code is
        kept in \texttt{final/extra/} for reference.

        \section{Final Architecture}
        Putting the pieces together, the final pipeline is:
        \begin{enumerate}
            \item \textbf{BiRefNet} background removal applied once to the
                  whole dataset (preprocessing).
            \item \textbf{Pose clustering} on DINOv2 global descriptors,
                  saved per image (preprocessing).
            \item \textbf{PatchCore} with DINOv2 features, PCA-512 and a
                  $10^5$-anchor coreset, post-processed with morphological
                  opening and small-blob removal.
            \item \textbf{U-Net} (resnet34 encoder, attention gates,
                  view-id FiLM) trained on synthetic anomalies generated by
                  the \texttt{ShapeTextureBank} with pose-aware CutPaste, BCE
                  + Dice loss masked to the foreground, TTA + multi-view
                  amplification at inference.
            \item Per-class \textbf{weighted-sum fusion} (Eq.~\ref{eq:fusion})
                  with $\alpha$ selected on labelled validation, followed by
                  a final $\sigma{=}0.9$ Gaussian smoothing.
        \end{enumerate}

        \section{Discussion}
        Table~\ref{tab:per-class-ap} shows three decisions that
        disproportionately moved the metric: \textbf{(i)} replacing ImageNet
        features with DINOv2 inside PatchCore, \textbf{(ii)} masking out the
        background with BiRefNet end-to-end (visible in the U-Net$^-$ vs
        U-Net rows), and \textbf{(iii)} calibrating raw PatchCore scores by
        the 99th percentile of per-image maxima on normal training
        images, which maps k-NN distances to a comparable $[0,1]$ range
        across classes. (i) and (ii) attack the same failure mode --- the
        model spending capacity on pixels the metric does not reward --- while
        (iii) fixes the incomparability of raw distances. The ensemble adds
        a smaller but consistent gain on top, mostly where one branch is
        dominant and the other acts as a precision filter (see
        \texttt{outputs/qualitative\_combined.pdf} for representative
        examples). Per-class variance is large: some
        classes are essentially solved by either branch, while a few
        remain hard for both. The main residual failure mode is small,
        low-contrast defects: PatchCore underweights them because their
        features are close to the normal manifold and the U-Net underweights
        them because the synthetic distribution rarely matches them. A
        natural next step is to feed PatchCore's confidence as an extra
        input channel to the U-Net, so the supervised branch learns where
        to focus its capacity.
